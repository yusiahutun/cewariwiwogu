Anorexic woman overcame illness after her boyfriend wrote a list of reasons to live

September 2024 Â· 6 minute read

An anorexic dancer who exercised for six hours a day says her boyfriend saved her with a romantic list of reasons to live - which ended with a wedding proposal.

Emma Stokes, from Huntington, Cheshire, became dangerously thin, plummeting from a healthy 9st 2lbs to just 6st 7lbs, after her grandmother died in March 2013. She said she felt like she'd 'lost control' of everything.

The 25-year-old dance teacher would drag herself out of bed in the mornings and head straight to the gym before teaching up to four hours of dance.

Chris Weir wrote a heart-felt letter to his girlfriend Emma in a bid to help her recover from anorexia 

But she would eat only tiny portions of lettuce, vegetables and a single Weetabix with watered-down milk which added up to less than 600 calories a day.

For two years her worried partner Chris Weir, 26, helplessly saw the love of his life deteriorate before his eyes. 

Emma's periods stopped, size six clothes were baggy on her and she had to give up teaching dance because she was permanently tired.

She saw an NHS therapist and dieticians at Bowmere Hospital in Cheshire as an outpatient but claimed they refused to give her meal plans.

She said she also had cognitive behavioural therapy but it was ineffective and her weight continued falling by up to 2lb a week.

After 12 months her mother decided to take her to a private, specialist hospital instead.

The 25-year-old dance teacher would drag herself out of bed in the mornings and head straight to the gym before teaching up to four hours of dance, pictured in hospital being treated for anorexia  

Emma said Chris' emotional not and proposal was a 'massive turning point'. She added: 'I want to get better so I can get married'

Emma Stokes pictured left before the anorexia struck and right at the height of her battle with the illness

Emma, who battled bulimia in her teens, said: 'My body was just shutting down. I literally use to drag myself out of bed, teach a class, set an alarm and go to sleep and then repeat.

'When people say anorexia you think they eat nothing, but my problem was I was dancing for three or four hours a day and then going to the gym.'

She added: 'For breakfast I would have one Weetabix with a splash of milk and the rest water. For lunch I would have something like a plate of vegetables and lettuce. Then for tea I would have a piece of fish and salad.'

Gradually Emma cut out more food groups and upped her exercise regime. 

'If I was hungry, I would have naps throughout the day so I couldn't eat,' she said. I was living with my mum and she kept trying to feed me, but I have always been such a determined person that if I say I'm not going to do something, I don't do it.

'The treatment I received on the NHS wasn't helpful at all. No one understood why I wasn't getting better and I kept losing more weight.

'My family were always really supportive and encouraging but they were terrified I was going to deteriorate or die. They just wanted me to get better.'

In February last year Emma got a miniature sausage dog, Colin, to help with her recovery, pictured together with boyfriend Chris 

A recent picture of Emma as she begins to rebuild her weight and looks forward to her forthcoming wedding 

On Emma's 23rd birthday in April 2014 she was admitted to the private Priory Hospital in Altrincham, Cheshire as an outpatient and stopped receiving NHS care.

She said the help she got from dietitians and therapists there was much more effective and she began to tackle her fear of food.

In February last year she got a miniature sausage dog, Colin, to help with her recovery.

She was taking him out in the middle of the night when she tripped down the stairs and injured herself.

She was admitted to hospital in Cheshire and while there she caught a bug and over the next four days ate nothing but one crumpet.

But after being put on a drip she somehow regained her appetite and left hospital feeling that she wanted to eat again.

She said: 'It sounds dramatic, but I honestly think my body had just given up. I was put on a drip and I was so weak. I told my mum I thought I was dying. But whatever was in the drip sorted me out and I woke up feeling much better. I actually wanted to eat.'

Emma left hospital on March 7 2015 and when she got home Chris, a packaging designer, got down on one knee and handed her a piece of paper.

It was his list of ten reasons entitled 'Why I want Emma to fully recover' and, on the back, Chris had written: 'Emma Victoria Stokes. Will you marry me?'

Emma left hospital on March 7 2015 and when she got home Chris, a packaging designer, got down on one knee and handed her a piece of paper

Delighted Emma, who now weighs 7st 4lbs and is still gaining weight as an outpatient at the Priory, said: 'Obviously I said yes.'

She still attends sessions once a week and is now having eye movement desensitisation and reprocessing (ENDR), a type of therapy which tackles traumatic memories.

She said: 'Chris proposing was a massive turning point. I want to get better so I can get married.

'There is still a way to go to get to where I'm supposed to be. But I'm now back teaching again and I have just qualified as a gym instructor.

'I was always a keen runner and I've been able to start racing again, which is also a massive thing.

'I feel so much better. There are still foods that scare me, and I think that is always going to be the way.

'Every time I have bad thoughts I think about the bigger picture. My periods still haven't come back, but I want to have children one day so that motivates me. '

Emma and Chris plan to wed in August.
