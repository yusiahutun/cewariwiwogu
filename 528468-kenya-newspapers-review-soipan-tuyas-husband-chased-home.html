Kenya Newspapers Review: CS Soipan Tuyas Husband Says She Chased Him Out of Their Home

May 2024 · 4 minute read

On Saturday, November 18, the local publication widely reported the debate surrounding the KSh 17 billion oil deal that involves businesswoman Ann Njeri.

The dailies also had space for the National Cohesion and Integration Commission (NCIC) report that has painted a grim image of employment distribution in county administration.

1. The Star

According to the paper, former Narok MCA Stephen Kudate sensationally claimed that Environment CS Soipan Tuya chased him out of their matrimonial home when she was appointed to office.

Kudate said they were legally married, having observed all traditional marriage rites, but in September 2022, Tuya deserted her matrimonial home when she assumed office as CS.

Kudate, in response to an application filed by Tuya seeking upkeep and child maintenance, denied neglecting the children they sired together.

He claimed the CS deliberately excluded him from participating in their children's welfare.

"As the head of the family, I was a man of means who provided adequately, and she knows that I am jobless, but with the little I have, I effectively play my parental role," he said.

Tuya moved to court in October this year seeking custody of their two children and a total of KSh 3.1 million yearly for their upkeep.

2. Saturday Nation

The publication reported members of the Meru County Assembly expressed displeasure with their county boss Kawira Mwangaza's threat to dissolve the government and said they are ready to impeach her instead.

Some of the MCAs are, however, fearful that they might not be elected if the government is dissolved.

A fraction of 59 MCAs who are against Mwangaza's leadership style intimate that they would consider the petition by 22 members of the public, who are now seeking to impeach senior officials of the County Executive.

The county assembly representatives are in Mombasa for an induction workshop and also to deliberate on several issues, among them the two failed attempts to impeach Mwangaza.

The majority side of the assembly termed the move by Kawira as a toll order due to the procedures that might last for several months.

3. Saturday Standard

Self-proclaimed people’s watchman, Busia senator Okiya Omtatah alleged that the KSh 17 billion used to finance the purchase of the controversial oil had been sneaked into the 2022/23 budget by the Ministry of Energy and Petroleum and later withdrawn.

He said the genesis of the controversy started in the 2022/2023 financial year when parliament approved KSh 5.9 billion for the ministry, out of which KSh 5.2 billion was to be raised through the ministry’s operations.

The balance of KSh 732 million was to be sourced from the Consolidated Fund as provided for in 2022/2023 estimates of recurrent expenditure for the year ending June 30 2023.

Omtatah further claimed that Parliament then passed an inflated budget of over KSh 43 billion, which was an increase of 5,836% contrary to Article 223 of the Constitution, which limits such deviation to a maximum of 10% of the sum appropriated per programme.

In a signed press statement backed with extracts from various gazette notices and supplementary estimates for the recurrent expenditure, Omtatah claimed this was the money that was used to buy the contested oil, which was now being purified.

He challenged Energy and Petroleum CS Davis Chirchir to fully disclose how the KSh 17 billion withdrawn by his ministry from the consolidated fund had been used.

The senator claimed that an oil importer involved in the KSh 17 billion oil saga was a private financial enterprise funded by the ministry.

4. Taifa Leo

The Swahili daily reported the price of tomatoes in Kisumu had risen significantly to the point that many families can no longer afford this essential cooking ingredient.

Along with onions, tomatoes are used to enhance the flavour of various vegetables and dishes.

A survey conducted by the publication revealed that production and transportation costs had skyrocketed.

Rose Awuor, a resident of Manyatta, Kisumu, who works in the area, mentioned that in the past, they used to spend KSh 100 to buy tomatoes every Sunday, which would last for a week.

However, currently, her boss has to allocate KSh 600 exclusively for tomatoes every week.

Previously, four tomatoes cost KSh 50, but now they cost KSh 120. Despite the high prices, Awuor also complained about the small size of tomatoes in the market compared to the past.
