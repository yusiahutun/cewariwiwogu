Home-run legend Barry Bonds to face his Playboy mistress - and intimate sexual questions - in steroids perjury trial

All-time great: Barry Bonds, who holds a number of baseball records, including most home runs, faces court next week

Baseball home-run king Barry Bonds will face former teammates and an ex-girlfriend in court next week - when they testify in a trial over whether he lied about taking steroids.

The 46-year-old San Francisco Giants legend has pleaded not guilty to charges of perjury and obstructing the course of justice, which stem evidence he gave before a 2003 grand jury.

Bonds told the grand jury he did not knowingly use steroids or growth hormones. The grand jury was investigating The Bay Area Laboratory Co-Operative (BALCO), whose head has pleaded guilty to dispensing steroids to professional athletes.

The prosecution plans to call a number of Bonds' former colleagues, who have admitted taking steroids.

And lawyers against Bonds also plan to call his former Playboy-posing mistress Kimberly Bell - asking her about his mood swings as well as intimate questions about how steroids may have affected the size of his testicles.

The sparring match between 37-year-old Bell and Bonds' defence lawyer Cristina Arguedas may be one of the high points - or low points - of the trial. 

Prosecutors say Bell's testimony about Bonds' physique and temper, for example, point to use of performance-enhancing drugs.

But the defence will attempt to discredit Bell, saying that her 'rich sexual history' may explain Bonds' outbursts.

Ms Arguedas is expected to take on the delicate task of aggressively questioning Bell without making her appear a victim to jurors.

Walter Brown, a former federal prosecutor who is not involved in the Bonds case, said: 'Especially in San Francisco, jurors may be of [the] view that people have a right to pose in Playboy. If you're trying to suggest those things are bad, that could backfire easily.'

Into the record books: Bonds launches his 762nd career home run off of Ubaldo Jimenez of the Colorado Rockies at Coors Field, Denver, on September 5, 2007

Bonds told the 2003 grand jury he never questioned the flaxseed oil, vitamins, protein shakes and creams that his trainer, Greg Anderson, supplied him.

As a member of the San Francisco Giants, Bonds broke Hank Aaron's 33-year-old career home run record in August 2007, his last season in baseball.

Three months later a grand jury indicted him for perjury and obstruction of justice.

Robert Weisberg, a Stanford University law professor who has closely followed the case, said: 'The prosecution wants to make this a simple case and the defence wants to make it a complicated case.'

Professor Weisberg added than government prosecutors must show that Bonds made a 'factual' statement that he knew to be incorrect. If the defence could create enough confusion about the evidence, the jury would feel it could not convict beyond a shadow of a doubt.

For the prosecution: Bonds' former mistress Kimberly Bell, left, and former teammate Benito Santiago, right, will give evidence about alleged steroid use

Admission: Santiago, Armando Rios, left, and Marvin Benard, right, have admitted they took steroids, but Bonds' defence will say it does not prove that Bonds did so

The prosecution can call on athletes who can testify about getting performance-enhancing drugs from BALCO.

Jason Giambi and his brother Jeremy Giambi have agreed to say that they obtained the drugs while in the Bay Area playing for the Oakland Athletics, court records show.

Bonds' former teammates Benito Santiago, Armando Rios and Marvin Benard have offered similar testimony, prosecutors say.

Defence attorneys will try to show that these athletes' experiences have little bearing on Bonds.

Rory Little, a University of California Hastings College of Law criminal law expert, said: 'The defence is going to point out that no witness has personal knowledge. They will say "You took steroids but you don't know that Barry Bonds took steroids".'

The government will draw on other kinds of evidence, including testimony from Bell and one of Bonds' doctors, Arthur Ting, about changes in Bonds' physique and temper.

Bonds was one baseball's greatest players during a 22-year career in which he also set a single-season record with 73 home runs.

He was named the National League's most valuable player seven times but his peak years were clouded by suspicions of performance-enhancing drugs.
