She was the woman to whom Brigadier Andrew Parker Bowles turned when his then wife Camilla was being swept away by the Prince of Wales. But last night Parker Bowles was alone again after his charming second wife Rosemary, 68, died following a heroic battle against cancer.

Friends say the solicitor's daughter brought him tremendous joy after the unhappiness of his later years of marriage to Camilla.

'They were incredibly happy and Andrew has been a tower of strength for Rosemary throughout her years of treatment and all the setbacks they encountered,' says a family friend. 'Of course he is devastated, but they had some very fulfilling times together.' 

Sad loss: Brigadier Andrew Parker Bowles with his late wife Rosemary

The couple married almost 14 years ago, a year after the former cavalry officer ended his 21-year marriage to Camilla following the relentless publicity that had surrounded the revelations of her affair to the heir to the throne.

After years in the spotlight, Parker Bowles, now 70, gratefully slipped into the background. Only a few months after their marriage, he was canvassing for votes in Sedgefield, County Durham, where Rosemary's daughter-in-law Lizzie Noel was trying to get a seat in Parliament  -  her opponent was Tony Blair.

In the years after their marriage  -  and particularly since Camilla has become Duchess of Cornwall  -  Andrew and the two wives in his life have become close friends. Camilla is almost certain to attend Mrs Parker Bowles's funeral.

Last night, Rosemary's son Henry, a former soldier, spoke of the family's pride at his mother's courage. 'It was a long, long illness but she was immensely brave, and bore her troubles with great strength and fortitude,' he tells me.

'She was especially pleased to reach Christmas and to be able to see her new step-grandchildren [the twin boys born to Andrew and Camilla's daughter Laura on New Year's Eve].'

Mother of three Rosemary died at her home near Malmesbury, Wiltshire, on Sunday night. She was said by family members to have been 'amazing' in her last months.

The cancer first struck four years ago, and at the time the couple were optimistic about treatment because it had been caught early. Soon afterwards friends were being told that she had recovered, but a year later the condition returned and she has been bravely battling ever since.

 

Lord's lovechild is 'a nice surprise'

It is the kind of discovery that could break up a family. But when royal confidant Lord Glenconner was told he might have fathered a lovechild in his youth, he turned to his wife, Anne.

And it was Lady Glenconner, former lady-in-waiting to Princess Margaret, who encouraged him to take a DNA test to establish paternity.

Says Glenconner, who turned the Caribbean island of Mustique from a mosquito-infested swamp into a luxury resort: 'It was Anne who said: "If you are going to be serious about this, then you need to take a paternity test." So I did.'

As a result, he learned that he has a son, Joshua Bowler, a psychotherapist, after an affair with artists' muse Henrietta Moraes more than 54 years ago.

A year after the birth, he married Anne, daughter of the fifth Earl of Leicester, and they had three sons and twin daughters together.

From St Lucia, where he now lives, Glenconner tells me: 'When the results of the test came back, Anne was very pleased.

'In fact, she will probably meet up with him before I do as I am not planning on coming back to Britain.

'We both think it is a nice surprise  -  although I probably wouldn't have thought so 54 years ago.'

 

Coming to a cathedral near you: Britain's unlikeliest touring theatre troupe  -  broadcaster Sue Lawley, her TV producer husband Hugh Williams and dashing Tory MP Hugo Swire.

They will perform Old Thunder: An Evening With Hilaire Belloc at St Edmundsbury Cathedral, Bury St Edmunds, in March, before taking the act to Exeter.

And it is not the trio's first outing. 'Last year, Sue asked me to take part in some readings of Belloc's work for Budleigh Salterton Literary Festival,' says Swire.

'I have reminded the other two that I am a politician and there is a General Election coming up  -  but it is addictive, and such fun.'

 

Knickers contest given a twist

Feminist: Liz Fuller says the Miss Great Britain contest empowered her

Four years after being forced out of organising the Miss Great Britain contest, TV presenter Liz Fuller  -  herself a former winner of the pageant  -  has snapped up the rights to the spectacle. 

'I ended up buying it back for less than I paid the last time,' says Liz, 34, who originally invested in the contest when she went into business with fashion mogul Robert de Keyser. 

Their partnership ended in acrimony, with de Keyser paying Liz off. But now he has sold the licence to the socialite. 

'I have changed it so it is no longer just about Miss Beautiful but about Miss Personality,' adds Liz, who has been approached to turn August's pageant into a reality TV show. 

'I consider myself a feminist  -  doing beauty pageants was very empowering for me, and it can be for these girls.' 

However, I fear her plans may not sit well with traditionalists  -  her first move has been to abolish the bikini round...

 

Still mourning his beloved dog Buster, Lord Hattersley has begun the hunt for a new four-legged companion  -  but it has not been easy.

'I have been to 12 different centres trying to find a rescue dog to replace Buster,' he tells me at a lunch for The Oldie magazine.

'Unfortunately, they all seem to be the same type of dog as Buster. He was a Staffordshire bull terrier crossed with a bit of alsatian, and I deliberately want a different breed.

'I certainly don't want the new dog to remind me of Buster. But so far I've had no success in my search.'

 

Olivia's orgy ordeal

Fittingly, since her first foray into acting was at Oxford, where she was a member of the university drama society, judge's daughter Olivia Grant is about to make her name playing a student . . . at Oxford.

Straight-laced role: Actress Olivia Grant

The stunning redhead takes the role of Alice in reformed drug dealer Howard Marks's biopic Mr Nice, alongside Welsh heartthrob Rhys Ifans. However, party-loving Olivia insists her experience at Brasenose College was a million miles away from Marks's own days at Balliol.

'Howard makes out he charmed the dean at his college to such an extent that he could do what he wanted, but my experience was rather more monastic,' Olivia, 26, tells me. 'I certainly didn't experience any orgies  -  perhaps I was at the wrong parties!'

No wonder then that St Paul's-educated Olivia was shocked when she arrived at the first day of filming.

'On the script I was sent it said I was playing a straight-laced girl but that I would be in some "party scenes".  When I got to the set it had been reworded as "orgy scenes",' she adds.

'Given [director] Bernard Rose's reputation for improvising, I was pretty nervous. Luckily, all these girls turned up who had been hired to be naked. It was a great relief to me.'

David Cameron's plans to increase the number of female parliamentary candidates by imposing all-women shortlists is about to come under attack from a very unlikely source.

I can disclose that millionairess Margot James, the most celebrated gay woman in the Tory ranks, is to dramatically step into the fray by declaring against the policy.

Margot, who shares a Chelsea townhouse with TV producer Jay Hunt, will reveal her colours in an Oxford Union debate next month.

The Conservative candidate for Stourbridge in the West Midlands, where she has to overturn a Labour majority of only 400 to be elected, Margot has already shown her independent streak by speaking out about Cameron's plans to reward marriage through tax breaks.

'Margot is a free spirit and will certainly shake things up at Westminster,' says a friend.
